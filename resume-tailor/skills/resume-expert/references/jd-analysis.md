# Job Description Analysis Framework

The job description (JD) is a map to the hiring manager's priorities. Reading it carefully — and reading between the lines — is the foundation of every tailored resume.

## Layer 1: Surface Reading (What They Say They Want)

### Extract the Keyword Inventory
Go through the JD and categorize every meaningful term:

| Category | What to Look For | Examples |
|----------|-----------------|---------|
| Technical Skills | Hard skills, tools, languages, platforms | Python, Salesforce, SQL, Figma |
| Domain Knowledge | Industry or functional expertise | SaaS metrics, supply chain, HIPAA compliance |
| Methodologies | Ways of working | Agile, OKRs, A/B testing, Six Sigma |
| Soft Skills | Explicitly stated behaviors | "strong communicator," "bias for action" |
| Role Titles | What they call the function | "Growth Manager" signals different DNA than "Marketing Manager" |

**Priority signals:**
- **Bolded or repeated terms** → top priority
- **Listed first in a section** → most important to the role
- **In the job title itself** → core identity of the role

### Distinguish Required from Preferred
Parse the language carefully:
- "Must have," "required," "essential" → non-negotiable; address directly
- "Preferred," "nice to have," "a plus," "ideally" → bonus points; include if true
- "Experience with" → familiarity expected
- "Expertise in" → deep mastery expected
- "Ability to" → they're not sure how to measure it; match the phrase exactly

## Layer 2: Subtext Reading (What They're Really Saying)

### Role Context Signals
Analyze the JD for what it reveals about the team's situation:

**Signals of a growing/scaling team:**
- "Build from scratch," "establish," "create the playbook"
- "First [title] hire"
- Emphasis on systems-building and process-creation
→ **What they want**: A builder-type, comfortable with ambiguity, who thrives without structure

**Signals of a team in execution mode:**
- "Drive growth," "hit targets," "optimize performance"
- Specific KPIs mentioned (e.g., "120% of quota," "30-day activation rate")
- Emphasis on speed and delivery
→ **What they want**: An operator, not a strategist; focus resume on output and results

**Signals of a team dealing with complexity or dysfunction:**
- Heavy emphasis on "cross-functional collaboration," "influence without authority," "stakeholder management"
- "Ambiguous environment," "fast-paced," "evolving priorities"
→ **What they want**: Political savvy and resilience; highlight experiences navigating complexity

**Signals of a culture-conscious hire:**
- Extended sections on company values, mission, or team culture
- Asks about "passion for" the domain or problem space
- "Culture add" language
→ **What they want**: Genuine alignment with mission; connect the user's background to their "why"

### Seniority Calibration
Assess whether the role is positioned at the right level:

| Signal | Interpretation |
|--------|---------------|
| "Manage a team of..." | Requires people management experience |
| "Partner with executives" | Senior IC or above |
| "Report to VP/C-suite" | High visibility; executive presence matters |
| "Own the roadmap" | Requires strategic autonomy experience |
| "Support the team" | Likely IC role even if titled "Senior" |
| "3–5 years" range for a complex role | Often hiring at the high end of range |

### Company Research Integration
Pull external context to enrich the analysis:

1. **Recent news** — funding rounds, acquisitions, product launches, layoffs. A company that just raised a Series C is in hypergrowth mode; a company restructuring is tightening. Tailor accordingly.
2. **Glassdoor / Blind / LinkedIn reviews** — what current/former employees say about the culture, management style, and challenges. This reveals unwritten expectations.
3. **The hiring manager's background** — if findable on LinkedIn, what do they value? What's their career trajectory? People tend to hire in their own image.
4. **The team's LinkedIn profiles** — what does the existing team look like? What schools, companies, and skills are overrepresented? This signals what "culture fit" actually means here.
5. **The company's self-description** — how they talk in press releases, job posts, and on their website. Mirror this language in the resume.

## Layer 3: The Gap Analysis

After decoding the JD and researching the company, map the user's profile against requirements:

### Strength Mapping
For each required qualification, identify which accomplishment from the vault best demonstrates it. Look for:
- **Direct matches** — same skill, same context → lead with these
- **Adjacent matches** — different industry or scale but same capability → reframe the context
- **Transferable matches** — different function but same underlying skill → bridge with clear language

### Gap Handling Strategy
When the user is missing something the JD asks for:

| Gap Type | Strategy |
|----------|----------|
| Missing a specific tool | List adjacent tools + note "experience with similar platforms" |
| Missing a credential | Omit or note "in progress" only if actively pursuing |
| Missing years of experience | Lead with impact and scope, not tenure |
| Missing industry experience | Connect transferable domain knowledge; emphasize learning velocity |
| Missing a "must have" | Be honest with the user — this is a real risk; flag it |

**Rule**: Never fabricate experience. Reframe, contextualize, and bridge — but never lie. Beyond ethics, misrepresented experience fails in interviews and background checks.

## Layer 4: Competitive Positioning

### What Makes This Resume Win vs. Getting Filtered
After the gap analysis, consider: what's the competition likely to look like?

- For roles with high applicant volume (100+): ATS score and keyword density are critical — most resumes won't reach a human
- For specialized roles with few applicants: Human differentiation matters more — be distinctive
- For internal transfers or referrals: The reader already has context — lean into strategic narrative over keyword density

### The "Why You, Why Now" Frame
Every tailored resume should implicitly answer:
- **Why you?** — Your unique combination of skills, experience, and perspective
- **Why this company?** — Your genuine alignment with their mission and moment
- **Why now?** — The career progression logic that makes this next step inevitable

These don't need to be written out explicitly — they should emerge from how the resume is constructed.

## JD Analysis Output Format

When analyzing a job description, produce:

1. **Role Summary** — 2–3 sentences on what this role is really about and what success looks like
2. **Keyword Inventory** — categorized list of top 10–15 terms to target
3. **Company Intel** — key findings from external research and what they imply for positioning
4. **Strength Map** — which of the user's accomplishments best address each requirement
5. **Gap Assessment** — any meaningful gaps and recommended handling
6. **Positioning Recommendation** — suggested narrative angle for the summary and top bullets
